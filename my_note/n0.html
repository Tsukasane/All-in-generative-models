GMM

Different Gaussians are represented by different {πi,μi,ρi2} tuples, with parameters meaning weight, mean, and variance. GMM is a mixture of them. Each Gaussian can be seen as a cluster, with:

∑i=1nπi=1

 

If we are given that zk(a latent that sampled from a Gaussian) is from a cluster i, the likelihood of observing xk is given as,

P(xk|zk=i,μi,ρi)=N(xk|μi,ρi)

Thus, for a N component Gaussian mixture model:

P(x|{πk,μk,ρk2})=∑k=1Nwib(x|uk,pk)

Which indicates the probability of the generated x is sampled from each single cluster.

HMM

A probabilistic representation of a system it models. It is used for modeling linear problems involving time series or sequences.

The probability of reaching the next state is dependent on the transition function of the current state. Each state emits residues or symbols based on their symbol-emission probabilities. The Markov chain(state sequence) that led to these emissions are not observable.

VAE

  • Autoencoder: unsupervised training with unlabelled data

    Z=f(WX+b)
    L=||x−x^||2

    Z is a compressed important feature

  • Variational Autoencoders: generating data x from a unknown latent distribution z.

    Assume the data {xi}i=1N is generated by a true prior latent distribution z(a Gaussian) given by pθ(z) where θ are the parameters of the model.

    The true condition is pθ(x|zi) but intractable.

    pθ(x)=∫pθ(z)pθ(x|z)dz

    Because we don't know the pθ(x), the posterior density is also intractable.

    pθ(z|x)=pθ(x|z)pθ(z)pθ(x)

    However, we can approximate pθ(z|x) through an inference network qϕ(z|x) which allows us to derive a tractable lower bound which can be maximized by proper optimization.

    (Left) the inference network qϕ(z|x) | (Right) the generator network pθ(x|z).

    Their outputs are the means and the diagonal covariances.

    With pθ(xi) independent of z, the logarithm of the data likelihood can then be expressed as:

    Ez∼qϕ(z|xi)[log⁡pθ(xi)]=Ez[log⁡pθ(xi|z)pθ(z)pθ(z|xi)]
    =Ez[log⁡pθ(xi|z)pθ(z)pθ(z|xi)qϕ(z|xi)qϕ(z|xi)]
    =Ez[log⁡pθ(xi|z)]−Ez[log⁡qϕ(z|xi)pθ(z)]+Ez[log⁡qϕ(z|xi)pθ(z|xi)]
    =Ez[log⁡pθ(xi|z)]−KL(qϕ(z|xi)||pθ(z))+KL(qϕ(z|xi)||pθ(z|xi))

    Concept: KL divergence

    Then using term Ⅰ and term Ⅱ to get a tractable lower bound ϵ(xi,θ,ϕ)≤log⁡pθ(xi) with its gradient available.

    • term Ⅰ ⇒ uses reparameterization trick to make it differentiable & describes the reconstruction of input data.
    • term Ⅱ ⇒ the posterior distribution must be as similar as possible to the prior.
    • term Ⅲ ≥0

    Therefore, while training, we attempt to estimate the parameters θ′ and ϕ′ by maximizing ϵ(xi,θ,ϕ) as

    θ′,ϕ′=argmaxθ,ϕ⁡∑i=1Nϵ(xi,θ,ϕ)

GAN

  • VAE: not the most accurate; blurriness in the generated images

  • GAN: w/o the usual procedure of maximizing a log likelihood (term Ⅱ in VAE); doesn't require Markov chains.

    • Deep Convolutional GANs (DCGANs),
    • Fully Connected and Convolutional GANs (FCC-GANs)
    • Conditional GANs (CGANs)
    • Stack GANs (SGAN)

 

Important Concepts

The Kullback-Leibler (KL) Divergence

is used to measure the similarity between two density functions p(x) and q(x), which is always ≥0 and is 0 iff p=q. It is defined by:

D(p||q)=∫p(x)log⁡p(x)q(x)dx
